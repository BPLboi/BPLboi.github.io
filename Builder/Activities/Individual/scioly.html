Science Olympiad

Overview

I participated in Science Olympiad for two years (7th and 8th grade), and over those two years I participated in six events (Circuit Lab, Mousetrap Vehicle, Battery Buggy, Game On, Potions and Poisons, and Mission Possible).

For the 25 years before 2019, Coolidge Middle school had always won first place at the state competition. Then, in 2019, the first year that I was part of the Science Olympiad team, my middle school finally beat them! We really enjoyed going to nationals in 2019!

In 2020, the state and national-level competitions were cancelled due COVID, so I only competed at the Harvard Invitational tournament.

Circuit Lab

Event Goal: to analyze electrical circuits. Sometimes included a practical element.

I loved this event!!! It merged perfectly with my love for hobby electronics, and helped me understand more of the theory behind how electronic components work. Through the event, I learned basic circuitry concepts such as series/parallel circuits, Ohm and Kirchhoff's laws, and more.

Mousetrap Vehicle

Event Goal: to build a mousetrap-powered car that traveled a certain distance (distance revealed at the competition).

Though the power source for this vehicle was different, the design we picked was largely based off of the design we used for battery buggy the year before. This vehicle was also lots of fun to build!

Battery Buggy

Event Goal: To build a motorized car that traveled a specific distance along a curved path. Distance revealed at the competition.

This was by far the funnest Science Olympiad event I ever did, and I put a lot of effort into our buggy! The picture to the left shows what our buggy looked like near the states competition.

After the states competition, slow-motion videos of our test runs revealed that the largest source of the inaccuracy was skidding while the vehicle was slowing down. To combat this, we tested various different tire materials (we eventually settled on the material pictured in the mousetrap vehicle photo), reduced the amount of power going into the motor, and added weights to the vehicle. Additionally, we started using microfiber cloths to clean the floor before our runs.

In addition, we started utilizing more accurate methods to aim the vehicle. Using two posts (these can be seen in the mousetrap vehicle photo), we attached a rifle scope to the vehicle before starting it, so that we could aim it at a target we would place on the ground. Using extensive testing, we built large tables of precisely where to place the aiming target for various distances to make the vehicle end up as close as possible to the end point.

At nationals, we ended up doing fairly well thanks to my partner Jaime's good predictions for how slippery the floor would be relative to the one on which we had practiced (THANK YOU JAIME!!!)

Potions & Poisons

Event Goal: To answer questions relating to poisonous plants and animals, and basic chemistry

This was a cool event, and participating in it taught me basic chemistry and stoichiometry.

Mission Possible

Event Goal: to make a Rube Goldberg machine that delivered a golf ball to the top of a golf tee.

Unfortunately, no one on my team was particularly interested in this event, including my partner and I. At the Harvard Invitational, my teammate and I got 9th place out of 32 teams by dropping a golf ball onto a piece of paper; many other teams had lost more points from penalties and having large contraptions than they had gained by having more complex machines than my team.
